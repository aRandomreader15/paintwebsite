Interior Paint

Interior paint is typically used on interior room walls. You'll typically want to prime before you paint as it helps it adhere better. A gallon of paint has around 400 SqFt 's worth of one paint coat. You can find your square footage by taking the width and length of your wall and multiplying it. The resulting area is your square footage.
Also, certain paint jobs require "speciality paint". This is just paint that has been specifically made for that job. Kitchen and Bathroom paint for example will have the right finish as well as mildew resistant materials in it.

Finishes

You'll want a different finish depending on your room. The scale of finishes for paint goes like this: Flat, Eggshell, Satin/Pearl, Semi-Gloss, High Gloss.
Flat paint is less durable but shows little imperfections so it's typically used for ceilings. Eggshell is a happy medium that most rooms tend to use such as bedrooms, living rooms and dining rooms. The shinier paints (Semi-Gloss, Satin/Pearl) are more durable but also show imperfections easier. Satin paint is found in kitchens/bathrooms and hallways as they take more wear and tear. Semi-Gloss paint is used for doors and trim as they come into lots of contact. High Gloss is not recommended to be used as an interior wall paint.

Application methods

You can use multiple different tools to apply paint. You can use a brush, roller and even a sprayer to put paint on your walls. Just bear in mind what type of surface you're painting (rough, smooth etc) and pick an accompanying brush or roller to go with it. Typically the more rough a surface, the thicker the roller's nap and stiffer the brush should be. The opposite applies for smooth surfaces. Make sure your paint is fully dry before putting on a second coat. Check the side of the can or the SDS online if you're unsure of dry times, clean up methods, and more. Lastly, make sure your paint is well agitated before applying it. Agitating it can mean either going to the store and asking the paint associates to shake the can for you (recommended if you're using old paint) or stiring it with a stir stick. Agitating your paint ensures it is whole, and that the pigment hasn't seperated and settled at the bottom.

Clean up!

If you plan to use your brushes in the future, make sure to wash them with warm soapy water before the paint dries. Ensure that all of the paint has been removed, even the stuff you can't immediately see. If you get paint on a surface that you don't want paint to be on, you can use paint remover or scrape it when it dries.
